üîç Definition: What is a Compound Word?

A compound word is formed when two or more words are put together to create a new word with a completely different meaning. This helps make our language clearer and more interesting!

The new meaning may be very different from the words you started with. For example, "tooth" (a body part) and "paste" (a thick liquid) combine to make "toothpaste" (something you clean your teeth with!).

üèóÔ∏è How Are Compound Words Formed?

Compound words can be formed in several ways:

  • Noun + Noun (e.g., toothbrush, football)
  • Adjective + Noun (e.g., greenhouse, blackboard)
  • Verb + Noun (e.g., swimming pool, washing machine)
  • Noun + Verb (e.g., sunrise, rainfall)
  • Adjective + Verb (e.g., dry-clean, high-rise)
  • Preposition + Noun (e.g., underground, overcoat)

‚≠ê The Three Types of Compound Words

1. Closed (Solid) Compound Words üîí

These are written as one single word with no spaces or hyphens. They are the most common type, especially for words that are used a lot.

Explanation: The two original words merge completely to become one new, unified idea.

Examples:

  • Notebook
  • Toothpaste
  • Basketball
  • Bedroom
  • Sunflower
  • Haircut
  • Firefly
  • Newspaper
  • Blackboard
  • Raincoat

2. Hyphenated Compound Words ‚ûñ

These use a hyphen (-) to connect the words. We often use hyphens for clarity, especially when the words act together as a single adjective before a noun.

Explanation: Hyphens help us read the words as a single unit. For instance, in "well-known author," the hyphen shows that both "well" and "known" are describing the author together.

Examples:

  • Mother-in-law
  • Well-known
  • Part-time
  • Long-term
  • Twenty-one
  • Editor-in-chief
  • Check-in
  • Runner-up
  • Merry-go-round
  • Self-esteem

3. Open (Spaced) Compound Words üí®

These are written as separate words but they are understood to be one single concept or idea. They still function as one word!

Explanation: Although written separately, the words represent one thing. For example, "ice cream" is one dessert, not just some ice and some cream that happen to be next to each other.

Examples:

  • Ice cream
  • Post office
  • High school
  • Living room
  • Full moon
  • Coffee table
  • Real estate
  • Hot dog
  • Bus stop
  • Police station

üí° Additional Notes: Word Evolution

Did you know compound words can change over time? They often follow this path:

Open ‚Üí Hyphenated ‚Üí Closed

  • Example: "data base" ‚Üí "data-base" ‚Üí "database"
  • Example: "e-mail" often becomes "email"!

This happens as words become more common and familiar.
